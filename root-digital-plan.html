01CORE RULESOperating Rules (Global Constraints)

These four rules apply to every game in the plan. Violating them produces unreliable learning data — passive games, burnout, false-mastery readings.

1. Reach Constraint [non-negotiable]

Targets
  • 3 players: Reach ≥ 18 (Leder Games official 3P floor)
  • 4 players: Reach ≥ 21 (4P floor — not 17, that's the 2P number)
  • Practical rule: always include ≥2 high/mid Reach factions (Cat 10, Eyrie 7, Duchy 8, Hundreds 9)
Correction from v1: The original plan listed "minimum 17" for 4P. That's the 2-player floor, not the 4-player one. The 4P minimum is 21, and even that's tight — aim higher when possible.

2. Session Limits (Burnout Control)

  • Max 2–3 games per session
  • Max 2 consecutive games without a break
  • Stop even if performance is high — fatigue inflates wins, so a hot streak is the worst time to keep playing

3. Rotation Rule

  • No faction more than 2 times consecutively
  • Maintain exposure diversity — playing 5 Cat games in a row teaches Cat, not Root

4. Regression Trigger

If you experience
  • Unclear losses — "I don't know why I lost" — the diagnostic gate failing silently
  • Overwhelm — too many simultaneous systems to track

Action: remove the latest complexity layer (drop the newest expansion or revert to a smaller player count) and revert 1 phase.

Correction from v1: The original used a Devanagari-script word ("अस्पष्ट") here — almost certainly a translation artifact. The correct word is unclear.

02PHASE 0Orientation — Touch Every Faction

Goal: See each base-game faction execute its strategy at least once. Build a mental model of "what each faction does to win" before you have to play any of them seriously.

Plan v1 said 1–2 games. Reviews flag this as too short. v2 = 3–4 games. With only 1–2 you may have only watched 2 of the 4 factions actually play — not enough to understand their scoring models.

Setup

  • Complete the base game tutorial (built into Root Digital)
  • 3–4 AI games, 4 players (so all 4 base factions are present)
  • AI difficulty: Easy — you're observing, not competing
  • Map: Autumn (default). Other maps come in Phase 3.

Learning targets

  • Turn structure: Birdsong / Daylight / Evening — what fits in each phase
  • Movement & Rule: who controls each clearing and why it matters
  • High-level scoring model of each faction: what generates VP for them
  • Reach awareness: feel the difference between an "empty" board and a "tense" one
Diagnostic Checkpoint 0 — must answer
  • What generates points for each base faction (Cat, Eyrie, Alliance, Vagabond)?
  • What causes each faction's engine to fail?
  • Who is currently winning at any given turn (approximate)?
Fail: repeat 1 game and re-attempt the checkpoint.
Pass: proceed to Phase 1.

05SAFEGUARDSAnti-Pattern Control System

Three ways your data lies to you. If any of these appear, your training signal is contaminated — fix the root cause before continuing.

1. The Vagabond Trap

Symptom
You won 3 of your last 5 games as Vagabond and feel mastery is approaching.

Reality: Vagabond can win without teaching you Root. Their relationship-management and item-collection scoring is a different game from the four-corners power dance you need to master.

Validation gate: mastery requires winning as Cat or Eyrie, not just Vagabond. If you can only win as Vagabond, you're not yet competent.

2. Emotional Retaliation

After every attack you make, ask three questions:
  1. Was the target leading?
  2. Was the target about to score a burst?
  3. Or am I just punishing them for what they did to me last turn?
If the answer is #3, that's tilt. Reset your decision criterion.

3. Low-Interaction Failure State

Symptom
The game feels passive. Few combats, no contested clearings, factions racing in parallel without colliding.

Diagnosis: almost always one of:
  • Reach too low — count it: under 21 in 4P? Under 18 in 3P? That's the cause.
  • Faction mix is too "soft" — too many low-Reach factions (Alliance + Lizards + Corvids = 8). Need 2+ high-Reach.

A passive game produces no useful learning signal. End it, fix the setup, restart.

Bonus pattern (from reviews): Single-AI plateau

If you're winning consistently against a fixed AI difficulty, you're not improving — you're optimizing for that AI's flaws. Promote difficulty (see §13) or transition to humans (see §14).

06GATESDiagnostic Checkpoints (Overview)

Every phase ends with a checkpoint. You must pass before advancing. Passing means honestly answering the gate questions — not "I think I get it" but "I can articulate this without hesitation."

Checkpoint 0
After Phase 0
  • Faction scoring models
  • Engine failure modes
  • Identify the leader
Checkpoint 1
After Phase 1
  • Predict opponent moves
  • VP spike detection
  • Reach feel ("empty/tense")
  • Per-faction proficiency
Checkpoint 2
After Phase 2
  • Pre-emptive intervention
  • 2-turn prediction
  • Deck-driven vs engine-driven wins
Checkpoint 3
After each Phase 3 expansion
  • New faction win conditions
  • Identify weaknesses fast
  • Adapt strategy to mix
If you fail a checkpoint: trigger the Regression rule (§01) — revert one phase, drop the latest complexity layer, re-attempt.
If you pass: proceed. Don't overthink it. The gates exist to prevent skipping, not to gatekeep forever.

Honest self-assessment hack

Self-grading is unreliable. After each phase, write your answers to the checkpoint questions in your post-game log (§16). A week later, re-read what you wrote. If it sounds confused or contradictory, you didn't actually pass.

10Phase 3C — Marauder + Hirelings (~6 games)

Promoted from "optional" per review: The Marauder expansion isn't optional in any modern Root setup that includes online matchmaking. Hundreds and Keepers are common, and Hirelings are arguably the biggest learning-friendly addition since Riverfolk.

Two factions added

  • Lord of the Hundreds (Hundreds) — Reach 9. Persistent board pressure via mob warriors and warlord track.
  • Keepers in Iron — Reach 6. Objective play via relics and waystation chains.

Hirelings — the missing tool

What hirelings do

Hirelings are mini-factions you can add to any game without committing a player slot. They run on simplified AI rules and add interaction to the board.

Practical use for this plan: Hirelings let you boost Reach in low-faction setups without bloating the player count. Want to play 3P with two low-Reach factions? Add a hireling for ~5 Reach.

Hireling-augmented setups

  • 3P: Eyrie + Alliance + Lizards + Hireling-Cats = 7+3+2+~5 = 17 (3P ≥ 18 still tight — pick a higher-reach hireling)
  • 4P: Otters + Lizards + Vagabond + Alliance + Hireling-Hundreds-Promoted = 5+2+5+3+~5 = 20 (close to 21 — add a 2nd hireling if needed)
Hirelings are the workaround for the "I want to play this fun low-Reach combo but it'd be a passive game" problem. Add a hireling, hit the floor, play.

Marauder-specific learning targets

  • Persistent board pressure (Hundreds' mob mechanic)
  • Objective play (Keepers' relic chains)
  • Hireling integration without overwhelming the main board
Diagnostic Checkpoint 3 (Marauder)
  • Quickly read a mob warlord's threat level (Hundreds)
  • Block a relic corridor or waystation chain (Keepers)
  • Predict how a hireling will affect your turn-order options

11Per-Faction Drill Targets

The original plan said "play X faction" but not "with what specific learning goal." Reviews flagged this — without a per-faction skill focus, you can play 2 Cat games and learn nothing specific because the goal was just "play Cat." Bind a goal to each faction.

Base factions

Marquise de Cat
Drill: dominance setup + multi-build turns
  • Identify dominance suit by turn 3
  • Place recruiters in dominance clearings
  • Practice 2-build turns powered by Overwork
  • Mastery: trigger a dominance attempt and survive turn 1 of opposition
Eyrie Dynasties
Drill: Decree column risk management
  • Add only 2 cards per turn, never 3+
  • Use Move column as the dump (safest)
  • Plan one turmoil deliberately, not by accident
  • Mastery: 5+ actions/turn for 3 turns straight without turmoil
Woodland Alliance
Drill: card hoarding + big-turn execution
  • Reach 6 cards in supporters before your 2nd base
  • Stay 2nd or 3rd in score until ready
  • Plan a turn that scores 10+ VP from supporters → spread → organize chain
  • Mastery: win a game from 15 → 30 in your last 1–2 turns
Vagabond
Drill: relationship management (avoid the trap)
  • Aid each opponent at least once
  • Manage Hostile relations deliberately, not by accident
  • Score from 3+ different sources (quests, items, hostile attacks)
  • Mastery: winning is NOT the goal here — the goal is understanding the broader game while playing as Vagabond

Expansion factions (bind drills similarly when you reach Phase 3)

  • Underground Duchy: sway timing, prince/duchess pivot
  • Corvid Conspiracy: hidden-info management, plot-bluff balance
  • Riverfolk Otters: service pricing, fund cycling
  • Lizard Cult: outcast manipulation, sanctify chains
  • Hundreds: warlord track, mob deployment
  • Keepers in Iron: relic recovery routes, waystation chains

Per-faction proficiency gating (added per review)

Before advancing past Phase 1, you should have:
  • At least 1 win as Cat OR Eyrie (the "real" mastery factions)
  • At least 1 game per base faction where you can articulate why you won/lost

12Map Progression

Added per review: the original plan was implicit-Autumn throughout. All 4 Root maps play very differently — Lake especially changes movement rules (rivers + ferries).

Map progression by phase

  • Phase 0–2: Autumn (default, balanced, learn the game on this)
  • Phase 3A: introduce Winter (suit randomization on clearings)
  • Phase 3B: introduce Lake (rivers + ferries — major movement rule shift)
  • Phase 3C: introduce Mountain (passes / chokepoints, slowest movement)

Map effects on Reach

  • Mountain reduces interaction by ~10% (passes block movement)
  • Winter reduces interaction by ~5% (suits don't pre-bind to clearings)
  • Bump Reach targets +1–2 on these maps

Map-specific notes

  • Winter: craft / overwork from cards alone, hoard cards, delay workshops
  • Lake: sawmills NOT adjacent to rivers, stacks near landings
  • Mountain: seize a pass early, sawmills behind passes are near-invulnerable

14Phase 4 — Human Transition

Added per review: the original plan was AI-only. AI vs human is a fundamentally different game. After Phase 3, transition to humans.

Why AI alone caps you

  • AI has predictable patterns; human play has psychology
  • AI doesn't form coalitions, doesn't bluff, doesn't tilt
  • AI doesn't read the room — Phase 2 skills like "intervene before VP burst" require humans for full expression

Transition path

Step 1
After Phase 3
  • 1–2 games online matchmaking
  • Same setup rules (Reach, rotation)
  • Just observe how humans differ
Step 2
After Step 1
  • Practice table-talk: deflect aggression, propose truces
  • Read coalition signals
  • Manage emotional retaliation in YOURSELF
Step 3
Ongoing
  • Mix online + AI
  • AI for drilling new factions, humans for testing
  • This is where 50+ games turns into mastery

What humans add

  • Coalition formation — informal teams against perceived leaders
  • Tilt management — emotional retaliation goes both ways
  • Bluffing — pretending to be weaker / stronger than you are
  • Negotiation — proposing truces, trades, or temporary alliances

15Combat / Crafting / Timing — Micro-Skills

Added per review: these are real skills the original plan mentions implicitly but never isolates as drill-able subjects.

Combat micro-skill

  • Dice math: 2d3, attacker takes higher, defender takes lower (Guerrilla War flips for Alliance). Memorize the kill-probability table.
  • When to fight: after the cost-benefit changes — battle that enables a build > battle that just kills warriors
  • Stack thresholds: 1, 2, 3, 5 warriors each have different vulnerability profiles

Crafting micro-skill

  • Timing windows: craft when the suit clearing is uncontested. Fresh sympathy → craft same turn (Alliance trick)
  • Suit dependency: match craftable cards to your reliable craft suits — don't hold a fox-costing item if you only ever Rule mouse clearings
  • VP vs upgrade: 1 VP items are filler; persistent upgrades (Cobbler, Armorers) are S-tier

Timing micro-skill

  • Scoring spike anticipation: count opponent supporters / wood / acolytes; predict their next big VP turn
  • Pre-emptive intervention: knock down their setup the turn BEFORE the spike, not during
  • Coalition timing: know when offering a truce or coalition trade will land

16Post-Game Journaling

Added per review: the cheapest leverage in this plan, and the original missed it. Self-grading via memory after 8 games is unreliable. A 2-minute log per game is the fix.

Template

Per-game log
  • Date / phase / # games this session
  • Factions in play + Reach total
  • Who won. Final scores.
  • Why they won (one sentence)
  • One turn I'd play differently — and why
  • One thing I noticed about an opponent (a tell, a pattern)
  • Did I pass the per-faction drill goal? (§11)

Use Root Digital's game logs

  • Root Digital records every action of every game — review the log of the LAST game played
  • Find one inflection point (a turn where the leader changed, or you lost a key clearing)
  • Ask: "what would I do differently from that point?"

Weekly review (every 6–8 games)

  • Re-read your last 6–8 logs in one sitting
  • Look for repeating patterns: "I always over-march in Phase 1," "I forget Outrage triggers vs Alliance"
  • One weakness identified per week → drill it next session
Why this is leverage: the diagnostic checkpoints rely on accurate self-assessment. Without notes, you'll forget what happened in game 4 by game 8. With notes, your assessments stay honest.

17Challenges Mode

Root Digital's Challenges Mode is a puzzle library — specific board states with specific win goals.

When to use

  • After 12–15 total games (not before — you need basic fluency first)
  • Max 1–2 per session
  • Supplement only — never replace real games

Purpose

  • Tactical calculation drills — "given this exact board, find the winning sequence"
  • Pure mechanical practice — they don't teach strategic timing or threat assessment
What Challenges DON'T teach: threat assessment, table dynamics, coalition reading, opponent psychology. Don't substitute these for live games.

18Table Politics (Late Skill)

Added per review: the original plan treats Root as a solvable optimization problem. Vs AI that's fine. Vs humans, especially in 4P, Root is partly a negotiation game — and that's a separate skill.

What table politics actually means

  • Coalition theory: 2 humans agreeing informally to focus on a 3rd. Often unspoken.
  • Threat distribution: directing aggression away from yourself by re-framing threats
  • Truce negotiation: "I won't attack your border if you don't attack mine"
  • Bluffing: appearing weaker (to dodge attention) or stronger (to deter attacks)
  • Vagabond diplomacy: Aid trades, hostile relationships, the entire Vagabond faction is essentially diplomacy in mechanical form

When to start drilling this

  • Phase 4 (humans) is when this becomes essential
  • Pre-Phase 4: just be aware it exists; don't try to "solve" it analytically

The hardest political skill: managing yourself

  • Detect tilt — emotional retaliation in YOU, not just in opponents
  • Don't make threats you won't follow through on (you lose credibility)
  • Honesty in non-binding talk is a long-term asset

19Mastery Definition (Honest)

Reframed per review: the original plan's "mastery" criteria after 18–20 games are actually "competent baseline" criteria. Real Root mastery takes 100+ games. Set expectations honestly.

Competent (after the 18–20 game plan)

You're playing competently when:

  • You track relative power, not just your own board
  • You intervene based on timing precision, not emotion
  • You can predict scoring turns before they happen (1 turn ahead)
  • You understand why every game outcome occurred (you can articulate it)
  • You've won at least once as Cat or Eyrie (not just Vagabond)

Mastery (50–100+ games)

  • 2-turn predictions consistent across factions
  • Coalition reads in < 2 turns of human play
  • You can play any faction at competent level (not just your favorites)
  • You can identify low-Reach failure-state setups before turn 1
  • You understand the meta of your local group / online tier

True mastery (~200+ games)

  • You can win 1v3 (you + handicap vs 3 strong players) some fraction of the time
  • You teach others without confusing them
  • You see asymmetric mismatches and adapt instantly
The honest read: finishing this plan makes you competent. Mastery is a separate, longer journey. Don't confuse "I finished the plan" with "I'm good at Root."

20Glossary

Terms used throughout this aid. Reference when stuck.

Plan-specific terms

Reach
Official Leder Games rating of how aggressively a faction interacts. Sum of all factions' Reach must meet a player-count floor or the game becomes passive (low-interaction failure state).
Phase (0–4)
Sequenced learning blocks. Each phase has a goal, setup constraints, learning targets, and a diagnostic checkpoint.
Diagnostic Checkpoint
Pass/fail gate at the end of each phase. Failure triggers the regression rule (revert one phase).
Regression Trigger
Conditions (unclear losses, overwhelm) that force you back one phase. Prevents pretending to be ready when you're not.
Vagabond Trap
Anti-pattern: winning as Vagabond without learning Root. Vagabond's relationship-management scoring sidesteps the core systems. Mastery requires Cat or Eyrie wins too.
Low-Interaction Failure State
Anti-pattern: a passive, low-conflict game. Almost always caused by Reach < floor. Diagnose, fix, restart.

Root Digital terms

Local Play
Root Digital's hot-seat mode where you control all factions yourself. Used in this plan only for System Debug Tasks (≤10 min) — too slow to use as your main mode.
Challenges Mode
Puzzle library — specific board states with win goals. Tactical calculation drill, not strategic learning.
AI Difficulty (Easy/Medium/Hard)
Three AI tiers. Easy = textbook, Medium = competitive, Hard = near-optimal opening lines. See §13 for phase mapping.
Online Matchmaking
Phase 4 — playing humans via Root Digital matchmaking queue. Adds psychology, coalition, bluff dimensions.
Hireling
Marauder-expansion mini-faction. Adds board interaction without consuming a player slot. Use to boost Reach in low-faction setups.

Root mechanical terms

Birdsong / Daylight / Evening
The three turn phases for every faction. Different factions do different things in each — see faction-specific aids.
Decree (Eyrie)
Eyrie's stack of mandatory promised actions. Failing to fulfill = Turmoil.
Turmoil
Eyrie's failure state. Discard Decree, lose VP per bird card in the discarded Decree, demote leader.
Sympathy / Outrage
Alliance mechanics. Sympathy = scoring tokens. Outrage = Alliance gets a free card when sympathy is removed/battled.
Outcast Suit / Sanctify
Lizard mechanics. Outcast = the suit Lizards get bonuses on each round. Sanctify = replace an enemy building with a Lizard garden.
Sway (Duchy)
Underground Duchy mechanic. Spend cards to add a card to a minister's slot; ministers grant ongoing abilities and contribute to the Crown Track.
Plot (Corvid)
Corvid Conspiracy mechanic. Face-down tokens with secret effects. Bluffing/scouting around plots is core Corvid gameplay.
Dominance Card
Special card that swaps the win condition: hold 3 corners / all 3 of one suit / 5 specific bird clearings instead of reaching 30 VP. Cats win 2/3 of their games via these.
Coalition
Late-game move: play a Dominance card to team up with the lowest-score player. They win if either of you hits the win condition. Used to neutralize threats like the Vagabond.
Field Hospitals (Cat)
Cat ability. When a warrior dies, discard a card matching the clearing's suit to revive at the Keep clearing. Lose the Keep clearing → no revives → faction collapses.
Guerrilla War (Alliance)
Alliance ability. When defending, you take the HIGHER of the two dice rolled (instead of the lower). Makes attacking the Alliance very expensive.
