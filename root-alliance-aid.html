01PRIORITY 1Be Invisible

If anyone at the table says "we need to stop the Alliance," you've already lost.

The big idea: The Alliance scores points by spending cards. Each card you spend on placing sympathy is worth a bit less than 1 VP. So the more cards you accumulate, the more VP you can dump out in a single turn later. The catch: that path is obvious to opponents once they notice you doing it. So your job is to look harmless while quietly stockpiling cards, then unload everything in one turn near the end.

TL;DR. Stay quietly in 2nd or 3rd place. Hoard your supporter cards. Then in a single turn, place a flurry of sympathy and watch your score jump 10–15 VP at once.

What's actually different about Alliance

Other factions score steadily — Cats from buildings, Eyrie from roosts, Lizards from gardens. Alliance scores in bursts. A Cat at 22 VP looks dangerous. An Alliance at 22 VP with 7 cards in supporters is also at 22 VP — but is one good turn away from 32. Other players don't always see this, and that's your edge.

Three things that get you killed

Threat 1 Score lead. Once you hit 1st, the table coalitions against you. You're now the boss enemy.
Threat 2 Visible card hoard. "Wait, she has 9 cards in supporters? That's like 9 free VP. Kill her base."
Threat 3 Provoking the Cats early. They have 5x your warriors and can park 4 of them in your base clearing for free, choking you off completely.

How to actually be invisible

  • Score-pace: stay just behind whoever's leading. If you're about to pass them, hold off and place a low-VP sympathy or just don't spread that turn
  • Visible footprint: 1–2 bases and a thin spread of warriors. Doesn't look threatening even when it is
  • Table talk: "I'm fine, I'm last in score, you guys handle the Cats" — players will believe you because the math agrees with them
  • Direct opponents elsewhere: when you place sympathy on the Eyrie's path, frame it as "I'm just messing with the bird, not picking on you"
Picture this
Turn 6: Cats are at 18 VP, Eyrie at 16, you're at 14 with 6 cards in supporters and Armorers crafted. The table is arguing about whether to focus on the Cats or the Eyrie. Nobody is looking at you. Two turns later you place 4 sympathy in one Birdsong, score 9 VP off them, and craft a 2-VP item — and you're at 25 with the table scrambling. That's the Alliance game.

02PRIORITY 2Anatomy of a Turn

Every Alliance turn has three phases. Each phase has specific actions you can take. Knowing what fits where unlocks combos like "place sympathy now, craft using its suit later this same turn."

Birdsong
Start of turn
  • Revolt in any clearing with sympathy + 2 supporters of that suit (places a base + recruits warriors + scores VP)
  • Spread sympathy to one new clearing per supporter card spent (matching suit, OR bird card)
  • (Craft Royal Claim if you have it)
Daylight
Middle of turn
  • Craft using sympathy clearings (1 craft per matching-suit sympathy)
  • Make any number of allowed actions: Move, Battle, Recruit, Organize (turn warrior into officer / sympathy)
  • Each non-Move action costs 1 officer to perform
Evening
End of turn
  • Draw cards (1 + 1 per sympathized clearing of a craftable suit, with the Draw Bonus)
  • Add cards from hand to your supporters stack if you want to bank them for later VP

The killer combo

Sympathy → Craft Place sympathy in a rabbit clearing during Birdsong. Then in Daylight, craft a rabbit-suit card using that same clearing. You've used the new sympathy before opponents can remove it.

Things to remember

  • Outrage trigger: any time an opponent removes one of your sympathies or battles in a clearing with your sympathy, they hand you a matching card. Free cards = free VP later.
  • Martial Law: if a clearing has 3+ enemy warriors, you can't spread sympathy there (unless you spend an extra card). This is your main constraint when targeting Cat clearings.
  • Order matters: Birdsong actions happen before opponents in Daylight, so a fresh sympathy token can survive the turn if you craft off it immediately.

03PRIORITY 3First Base — Land It Safely

Goal of turns 1–2: get one base onto the board, alive. Why it's everything: until you have a base, you're not really a faction yet — you can't recruit warriors, you can't replace lost ones, and your scoring engine is tiny. Once a base exists, all the other stuff (officers, organize chains, sympathy spread) becomes possible.

TL;DR. Look at all 6 of your cards (3 hand + 3 supporters), pick a suit you have 2+ of, find a safe-ish clearing of that suit, drop sympathy turn 1, revolt turn 2.

Step 1 — Evaluate your cards

You start with 3 cards in hand and 3 cards in your supporters stack (face-down pile of "future actions"). Look at all 6 together.

  1. Group by suit: count how many fox / rabbit / mouse / bird cards you have
  2. Find your "revolt suit": a suit where you hold 2+ matching cards (revolt costs 2 supporters of the matching suit)
  3. Bonus points: if you have a 3rd card of that suit OR a bird card, you have a backup revolt in case the first clearing gets stomped
Why two opportunities? Opponents can pre-empt you turn 1 by clearing the sympathy or putting warriors there. If you only have one revolt option and they neutralize it, you're stuck without a base for an entire round — the Alliance equivalent of falling off a cliff.

Step 2 — Pick the clearing

You want a clearing that's safe enough to survive turn 1 with sympathy on it, then strong enough to host your base going forward.

Look for
  • Central, well-connected (3+ paths) — gives you outflow routes for spreading sympathy later
  • No or few enemy warriors / buildings already there
  • Far from the Cat Keep and the Eyrie home roost
  • Matches your "revolt suit" (so the cards you've banked actually work there)
Avoid
  • 2-path corner clearings — easy to bottleneck you in. You have nowhere to spread.
  • Clearings adjacent to enemy strongholds (Cat home, Eyrie roost) — they can park warriors there for free
  • Clearings Vagabond/Riverfolk view as easy VP targets
Why central not corner? Some online guides recommend corners "because they're isolated and hard to reach." The BGG strategy guide disagrees: corners with only 2 paths give the Cats a perfect choke point. Central clearings give you the freedom to spread sympathy outward in any direction. Yes you're more visible — but visibility you can manage; bottlenecking you can't.

Step 3 — Execute the opening

Example turn 1
Birdsong: Place 1 sympathy in a rabbit clearing (your revolt suit).
Daylight: Craft a rabbit-suit card if you can — Armorers if it's there. Otherwise skip.
Evening: No moves needed yet; put any remaining hand cards into your supporters stack.

Turn 2 Birdsong: Spend 2 rabbit supporters → revolt. Base placed, warriors recruited (1 + 1 per other Alliance clearing), 1 VP scored. Now you're a real faction.
Hold revolt for T2 unless...
  • You have a favor card in hand (game-changing when triggered with full sympathy setup)
  • You have Ambush matching the clearing's suit (defensive surprise)
  • You can craft S-tier upgrades (Armorers, Better Burrow Bank) only by revolting now

04PRIORITY 4Armorers + Guerrilla War — The Engine

Guerrilla War is the single strongest faction ability in Root.

What Guerrilla War actually does

In Root, combat works like this: both sides roll two dice, getting numbers from 0 to 3. The attacker normally takes the higher roll as their hits, and the defender takes the lower roll. So if you roll 3 and 1: attacker scores 3 hits, defender scores 1 hit.

Guerrilla War flips this for the Alliance. You always take the higher number when defending — even though you're the defender. So opponents who attack you take the bigger of the two dice as hits to themselves, and you take the smaller. Attacking you is suddenly very, very expensive.

Why this is huge. Other factions need to commit big stacks of warriors and good rolls to clear opposing units. Against the Alliance, opponents need even bigger stacks to absorb the higher-die return fire — and your rolls don't even need to be lucky. You just need to be there.

The probability table — defender losses when attacked

Assume opponent attacks you in a clearing where you have at least 1 warrior. Of the 16 possible (die1, die2) combinations:

44%
0 losses to you (7 of 16)
31%
1 loss (5 of 16)
19%
2 losses (3 of 16)
6%
3 losses (1 of 16)

What this means for your bases and sympathy

Sympathy + 1 warrior Opponent has only a ~25% chance to fully clear it in a single battle. 75% of the time, sympathy survives.
Sympathy / Base + 2 warriors Drops to ~6% chance of losing both. Opponents need multiple battle actions to break through.
+ Armorers Armorers lets you discard a card to cancel all enemy hits in one battle. Suddenly even guaranteed-bad rolls don't kill your stuff.

Why Armorers is mandatory

Armorers is a fox-suit upgrade that, when crafted, lets the Alliance ignore an entire batch of incoming hits in exchange for one discard from hand. Combined with Guerrilla War, opponents have to pay both in dice odds and in extra commitment to even attempt a kill. The math just stops working for them.

  • If any other faction had Guerrilla War + Armorers, they'd never lose a game
  • Once Armorers is on the table, opponents look at your base and say "ugh, not worth the trouble" and pick on someone else
  • Even better: stack with Sappers (negate one hit during combat), and bases become genuinely untouchable
Picture this
Cats player commits 5 warriors and 2 actions to wipe your base of 2 warriors + Armorers. Best case for them: ~50% they roll well enough to deal 2+ hits, and you cancel one with Armorers. Even if they break through, they've spent a turn doing nothing else — meanwhile you've gained a card from Outrage when they battled you, and you'll just rebuild next turn. The trade is great for you.

05PRIORITY 5The Big Turn — How You Actually Win

15 → 30 VP in one turn is the Alliance win shape.

Other factions grind out 1–3 VP per turn for ten turns. You don't. Instead, you hover at around 15 VP for most of the game, slowly accumulating cards in your supporters stack. Then in one explosive turn near the end, you spend 6–8 cards on sympathy and craft, scoring 10–15 VP at once.

TL;DR. Don't try to score steadily. Bank cards, set up clearings, then in one turn: spread a wave of sympathy, craft off it, and organize warriors into more sympathy. Score 10+ VP in a single Birdsong→Evening sequence.

The sympathy VP track

The Alliance sympathy track is non-linear: the first sympathy of a suit scores 1 VP, the second scores 1, then it ramps up. The 8th, 9th, and 10th sympathy of a suit each score 3 VP. This is "the 3-cost sympathy track" — most of your win condition lives here. Setting up the early/cheap sympathies first, then dumping the late ones in one turn, is what gives you the burst.

The sequence (one specific turn)

Birdsong Spend 3–4 cards from supporters to spread sympathy across multiple clearings (revolt 1 if possible to add a base + score VP)
Daylight Craft using the freshly-sympathized clearings — pull favor cards or VP items
Evening Move base defenders out, organize warriors → fresh sympathy on more clearings

Setup requirements (must all be true before you "fire")

  • 1–2 bases secure, ideally with Armorers crafted (so the burst doesn't lose your bases mid-explosion)
  • 5+ supporter cards stockpiled, mixed across 2–3 suits (gives you flexibility to spread to multiple suit types)
  • Clearings of 2–3 different suits within your reach (so you can spread broadly, not just into one suit)
  • 2+ officers ready (so you can chain Move + Organize in Daylight)

Score milestones — when to do what

  • 0–10 VP: Invisible. Just place sympathy cheaply, hoard cards, get Armorers down.
  • 10–20 VP: Building. Drop a 2nd base if you have a great spot. Keep stocking supporters.
  • 20–25 VP: Load the cannon. Don't push score yet — set up clearings and stockpile a final 5–6 cards.
  • 25+ VP: Fire it. Big-turn execution; race to 30 in one turn before the table can react.
Worked example: 17 VP → 30 VP in one turn
You're at 17 VP with 7 cards in supporters (3 rabbit, 2 mouse, 2 bird). You have sympathy on 5 rabbit and 4 mouse clearings already.

Birdsong: Spread sympathy to 6th, 7th, 8th rabbit clearings using 3 supporters. The 8th rabbit scores 2 VP. Spread to 5th mouse clearing → 1 VP.
Daylight: Craft a Favor of the Mice (2 mouse + bird) → wipes 4 enemy warriors and scores. Tax Collector if up. +3 VP from craft.
Evening: Organize 2 warriors into 9th + 10th rabbit clearings → both score 3 VP each = 6 VP. Total: ~13 VP. You're at 30, you win.
Don't telegraph Don't let your supporter pile cross ~8–9 cards visibly. The card count is in front of you and observant opponents will count it. ~6 cards is "no big deal." 9+ rings alarm bells.

06PRIORITY 65 Failure Modes (and What to Do Instead)

If you've lost a game as Alliance, it was almost certainly one of these. Fix them and your win rate jumps.

1 Visible threat — taking the score lead
The moment you cross into 1st, the entire table coalitions against you. Cats blockade your bases, Eyrie battles your sympathy, Vagabond goes Hostile.
Instead: If you're about to take the lead, place a low-VP sympathy or skip the spread that turn. Park at 2nd or 3rd until your big-turn setup is ready.
2 Burning every card on Martial Law
Martial Law (sympathy in 3+ enemy warrior clearings) costs an extra card. Many beginners burn through their supporters trying to spread sympathy through Cat-occupied clearings, leaving themselves with no late-game burst.
Instead: Bank cards. Spread to easier clearings. Only fight Martial Law when the target clearing is unusually valuable (favor card setup, wood stockpile, etc.).
3 Spreading thin — too many officers, too few defenders
Every warrior you "promote" to officer is one less defender at your base. Lose the base, lose Field Hospitals — er, you don't have Field Hospitals, lose the base period. Game over.
Instead: Floor of 2 warriors per base, always. Cap officers around 5 (more brings diminishing returns).
4 Buying from Riverfolk
Riverfolk services let them keep your warrior on their score sheet. Your army size shrinks. You lose Guerrilla War's "we have warriors everywhere" advantage for a one-time card or service.
Instead: Never. Buy. Anything. From. Riverfolk. There is essentially no exception.
5 Letting Lizards sanctify your base
When the Lizards' "outcast suit" matches your base's clearing suit, they get a massive discount on attacking you. Lose your base to a sanctify chain and your engine is dead.
Instead: Stay on ONE base. Burn cards aggressively to manipulate the outcast suit (you cycle more cards than them). See Lizard Counter.

07Crafting Priorities

Why crafting matters for Alliance: unlike the Cats (who need workshops on specific suits), the Alliance can craft using any sympathy clearing. You only need a card matching the suit of one of your sympathy clearings. This makes you the most flexible crafter in the game — and certain crafts are so good for the Alliance specifically that they redefine the matchup.

TL;DR. Lean mouse and rabbit for VP cards (avoid fox — those are the Vagabond's gear). Prioritize Armorers > Better Burrow Bank > Tax Collectors > Sappers. Use the Birdsong → Daylight trick to craft using fresh sympathy.

S-Tier Upgrades — craft these if you can

Armorers What it does: Discard a card to negate all hits in one battle.
Why it's mandatory: Already explained in §04. Combined with Guerrilla War, opponents stop attacking you because the math doesn't justify the cost.
Better Burrow Bank What it does: Once per turn, draw a matching-suit card.
Why for Alliance: You convert cards into VP faster than any other faction. Each free card BBB gives you ≈ a free 1 VP.
Tax Collectors What it does: Spend a warrior + take an action → draw a card.
Why it's good even though you're low-warrior: The card is more valuable to you than the warrior. Plus you can recruit a replacement at your base in Evening for free.
Sappers What it does: Negate one hit per battle when defending.
Why: Stacks with Armorers and Guerrilla War. Three layers of defensive math = practically unkillable bases.

Anti-Ambush Tools

Ambush cards let opponents deal hits before you roll. Bad news for Alliance — your Guerrilla War advantage doesn't help against pre-roll hits.

Scouting Party (mouse) Lets you ignore Ambushes entirely. Important when you're forced to attack (occasionally you'll need to clear a sympathy-removing piece).
Codebreakers (mouse) See an opponent's hand. Now you know whether they have an Ambush before you commit — invaluable scouting.

The Sympathy-Before-Craft Trick

Order of operations
  1. Birdsong: place sympathy in a rabbit clearing
  2. Daylight: craft a rabbit-suit card using that clearing's sympathy as the resource
  3. Opponents can't remove the sympathy first because Daylight comes after Birdsong on your turn
Why this is powerful: You can plan a craft for a specific suit, place a sympathy of that suit on this exact turn, and craft the card before anyone interferes. This lets you set up favor cards (which require multiple sympathy of one suit) and use them the same turn you place the last token. Multi-turn setup, single-turn execution.

VP Cards — Mouse and Rabbit Only

  • Mouse VP cards (Mouse-in-a-Sack, Codebreakers, Scouting Party) — safe, fits Alliance synergy
  • Rabbit VP cards (Cobbler, Smuggler) — also safe
  • Avoid fox VP cards — those are the best Vagabond gear (Crossbow, Sword), and you don't want to craft them onto the table where the Vagabond can grab them and use them against you

08Sympathy Math & Placement

The core trade. Every supporter card you spend to place sympathy is roughly worth 1 VP (sometimes more thanks to the 3-cost track). If an opponent destroys that sympathy, you get a card from Outrage. So your VP is largely guaranteed: either the sympathy stays and scores VP, or it gets removed and you replace the card you spent. Heads you win, tails you also win.

TL;DR. Sympathy is cheap for you (1 card) but expensive for opponents (whole battle action) to remove. Place sympathy where opponents have to go anyway — Outrage gives you free cards every time they pass through.

Maximizing VP per card spent

  • Match supporter suits. You spread sympathy by spending a supporter card matching that clearing's suit. So spread to clearings of suits you actually have in your stack — don't try to force a rabbit spread when you have only mouse supporters.
  • Bird cards are wildcards. They count as any suit when spending. Save them for: same-suit-as-base spreads (the Lizard counter), forcing a spread when no matching cards exist, or filling a favor card requirement.
  • Hunt "free card" placements. Sympathy on a clearing the Eyrie's Decree forces them to battle/move through = guaranteed Outrage trigger = guaranteed free card. Best ROI in the game.

Provocation patterns

Sympathy as bait Place sympathy on clearings opponents have a strong reason to enter (Eyrie's forced-decree path, Cat's wood corridor, Vagabond's quest target). They either avoid it (paying tempo cost) or trigger it (paying you a card).
Why "destroyed" sympathy is still a win: You spent 1 supporter card to place it (~1 VP loss). Outrage gives you a matching-suit card back when destroyed (~1 VP earned). Plus the opponent spent an entire battle action to do it (~2 VP of opportunity cost). Net trade: opponent loses ~2 VP, you net ~0. Free damage on your enemies.

Special targets — places worth being aggressive

Cat wood stockpile When the Cats stockpile wood for a building burst, that wood is sitting in a clearing. Revolt there → wood is destroyed (massive Cat tempo loss) and you score base VP. Devastating swing.
Eyrie forced-Decree clearing The Eyrie's Decree forces them to battle/move/build in specific suits each turn. If you can plant sympathy where their Decree forces them, they hand you a card every turn until turmoil.
Lizard outcast-mismatch clearing Place sympathy in a clearing whose suit is NOT the Lizards' outcast suit. They can still attack it, but at full price (no sanctify discount).

How NOT to place sympathy

  • In a clearing with 3+ enemy warriors (Martial Law: costs +1 card to place, often not worth it)
  • Adjacent to your only base if it's not for a planned revolt — clusters all your tokens in one spot, creating a single point of failure
  • In Riverfolk clearings — they have nothing worth destroying and won't trigger Outrage usefully

09Officers & Bases

What an officer is, and why it matters

An officer is an Alliance warrior that you've promoted (by using the "Train" action with a matching-suit card at a base). Officers are not warriors — they don't defend, but each officer gives you +1 action in Daylight. Each non-Move action (Battle, Recruit, Organize) costs 1 officer-action.

So the tradeoff is sharp: every officer = +1 Daylight action but −1 base defender. Lean too hard either direction and you collapse.

TL;DR. Get to 2 officers fast (unlocks Move+Organize chains). Cap around 5 (more brings diminishing returns). Always keep at least 2 warriors at every base.

Officer Targets by Phase

  • Turns 1–3: rush to 2 officers. With 2 officers + Move you can move a warrior and organize it into sympathy in one Evening — the foundation of the time-bomb revolt.
  • Turns 4–6: climb to ~5 officers. Now you can chain spread, organize, and move multiple times in one turn — setting up the big turn.
  • Beyond 5: diminishing returns. Each additional officer = 1 less warrior defending. The 6th officer is rarely worth it.

Base Defense Floor

2 warriors + Armorers crafted The standard. Most opponents won't bother attacking — the math is awful for them (see §04). They'd need 4–5 warriors and 2 actions to maybe break through, while you replace whatever they killed in 1 Recruit action.
1 warrior + Armorers Tempting target. Acceptable for 1 turn while you reposition. Don't sustain this.
0 warriors at the base Free destruction — anyone can walk in and remove the base for 0 hits. Game over for you most of the time. Never let this happen.
Why 2 warriors specifically: Guerrilla War's defender-takes-higher-die means with 2 warriors, opponents need to inflict 2 hits in one battle to clear them. The probability of that is roughly 19% (vs ~25% to clear 1 warrior). Adding Armorers drops it further. From the opponent's perspective: ~80% chance they spent an action and got nothing.

Reserve a base spot for adjacency

Pro tip Don't rush placing your 2nd base. Wait until you find a clearing that's adjacent to one or two future revolt targets. Once that base is down, you can organize warriors directly from it into adjacent clearings, multiplying your action range.
Why adjacency matters
Say you want to place sympathy in 3 different mouse clearings on your big turn. If your base is adjacent to 2 of them, you can: 1) spread into adjacent clearing A, 2) Move warrior from base into A, 3) Organize warrior in A into more sympathy in clearing B. One base, three sympathies. Without adjacency, you're stuck with just direct spreads, half the throughput.

10Time-Bomb Revolt

The shift. Once your first base is down, your strategy pivots. Phase 1 was about safe sympathy in clearings nobody cares about. Phase 2 is about aggressive sympathy in clearings opponents actually want to hold — turning Guerrilla War + Armorers into a weapon, not just a shield.

The "time-bomb" pattern lets you plant a defended sympathy/base inside enemy territory in a single turn. Once it's there, opponents have a choice: ignore it (and watch you score off it), or attack it (and feed their warriors into your Guerrilla War meat grinder).

TL;DR. Birdsong: spread sympathy into an important enemy clearing. Daylight: move warriors there. Evening: recruit one back at base so home stays safe. Result: a tank-defended thorn opponents can't easily remove.

The Pattern — Step by Step (needs 4+ officers)

  1. Birdsong: spend a supporter card to spread sympathy into a key enemy clearing
  2. Daylight (action 1): Move 2 warriors from your base into that clearing
  3. Daylight (action 2): if you also have 2 supporter cards of that suit, Revolt — converts the sympathy into a base, kills enemy buildings/warriors, scores VP, recruits warriors
  4. Evening: spend 1 officer-action to Recruit a warrior back at your home base to maintain its 2-warrior floor

Why this works against the math

From the opponent's perspective: they now have an Alliance base/sympathy in their territory with 2+ warriors and Guerrilla War. To remove it, they'd need to commit several warriors and 1+ actions, and the dice math says they'll lose more than they gain. From your perspective: you got a forward operating base for cheap.

Stack the math Add Ambush + Armorers + Sappers on top, and that 2-warrior base might literally be unkillable in one turn. Opponents end up wasting 2–3 turns trying to break through, scoring nothing — and feeding you Outrage cards every time.

Where to plant the time bomb

  • Eyrie's forced battle/move/build clearing — guaranteed Outrage card stream every turn until they turmoil
  • Cat wood stockpile clearing — revolt destroys the wood (massive Cat VP swing) and gives you a forward base
  • Lizard outcast-mismatch clearing — denies them sanctify discounts on your tokens
  • Vagabond quest clearing — slows their gear progression while you score

How to read whether to commit

Green light You have 2+ supporter cards matching the target suit, your home base has 2 warriors after the move, and opponents lack a single-action way to clear it (no Brutal Tactics + Commander combo on the Eyrie, no Vagabond at full sword count).
Red light Opponent has Brutal Tactics + Commander (Eyrie can rip through it), or you'd be left with 1 warrior at your home base, or you don't have a follow-up plan if it survives 2 turns.

11Pre-Turn Audit

Run this 30-second checklist at the start of every Birdsong, before you spend any cards. Catches most mistakes before they happen.

1 What's my visible score rank?
If 1st: slow down. Place low-VP sympathy or skip the spread. Stay invisible.
If 2nd–3rd: proceed normally.
If last: you're invisible — push harder. Spread aggressively, set up the big turn.
2 Base defense floor met?
≥2 warriors at every base. Armorers crafted if possible. If a base is at 1 warrior, prioritize a Recruit action this turn before everything else.
3 Outrage card opportunities?
Look at the Eyrie's Decree. Are they forced into a clearing where you could plant sympathy? If yes, that's a free card every turn — high priority spread.
4 Lizard outcast suit check (if Lizards in game)
What's the current outcast suit? Does it match your base clearing's suit? If yes, defend hard and use bird cards to spread elsewhere this turn.
5 Big-turn distance?
Count cards in supporters × ~1 VP each. That's your ceiling for the next 2 turns. If you're at 25 VP and have 6 supporters → fire the big turn now.
6 Threat scan
Anyone at the table loud about "stopping the Alliance"? Anyone holding 5+ cards (likely planning to attack you)? If yes: protect your most valuable base, decline obvious VP this turn.

12Matchups

Each matchup card lists: their threat to you, your counter, and (when relevant) the specific danger card or play to watch for.

Marquise (Cats)
Threat: Choke-out — they can dump 5+ warriors in your base clearing as a blockade.
Counter: Don't be their biggest threat. Reserve a 2nd base adjacent to revolt clearings for outflow paths. Look for wood stockpiles — revolting on those is a massive VP swing for you, massive loss for them.
Eyrie
Both threat and farm. Their static Decree forces them through specific clearings — plant sympathy there for an Outrage card-stream.
Migrate away as they grow strong. Commander negates Guerrilla War; Brutal Tactics + Commander = your ability nullified. Exit their orbit before that combo lands. Despot eats your sympathy for VP — they get points but feed you cards (calculate the trade).
Vagabond
Threat: Crossbow evens GW odds. Scoundrel's Scorched Earth = nuclear (once/game). Multi-attack turns.
Counter: Befriend them — craft weak items as Aid (mutually beneficial VP). Never craft fox VP cards (their best gear). Let bigger factions handle them. If they go Hostile you become their VP buffet.
Riverfolk
Minimal interaction. NEVER buy services — they keep your warriors permanently. You're terrible revolt targets for each other.
Action: Make sure someone at the table polices them. Talk down opponents who want to over-buy from Riverfolk.
Corvid Conspiracy
Plot bombs near your bases = catastrophic. Codebreakers helps you scout. Expose plots if you can spare actions; don't stack a base with everything you've got.
Duchy / Keepers
Aggressive military factions. Treat like Cats: don't be biggest threat, hold defensive layers, exploit their crown/relic obsession to plant sympathy on their forced paths.

13Lizard Counter (Key Matchup)

Lizards vs Alliance is the most consequential matchup in the game. Both factions can wreck the other if mismanaged. The good news: you have the initiative if you play it right, because you cycle more cards than them and can manipulate the suits.

The Lizard mechanics you need to know

Outcast suit
The Lizards designate one of the four suits as "outcast" each round. While that suit is outcast, the Lizards get bonuses on those clearings — cheaper attacks, more recruits, etc.
Sanctify
Lizards can spend acolytes to "sanctify" a clearing of the outcast suit, which lets them replace an enemy building with their garden. If your base is in an outcast-suit clearing, sanctify can convert it directly into a Lizard garden. Game-ending for you.
Acolyte
A Lizard "currency" piece. They get acolytes from killing enemy warriors. Acolytes pay for sanctify and other Lizard plays. Killing Lizards yourself doesn't give them acolytes.

Core Rule

Never let them sanctify your base. Stay on ONE base. Manipulate the outcast suit so it never matches your base's suit.

Why one base, not two

Each base you have is a sanctify target. If you have two bases of different suits, the Lizards can wait for either suit to become outcast and pounce. With one base, you only need to keep one suit out of the outcast slot — much easier to manage.

How to manipulate the outcast suit (your edge)

The outcast suit cycles based on cards being discarded. You discard more cards than anyone — every Outrage trigger feeds you cards, and you cycle supporters constantly. So you have unmatched leverage.

  • Spread sympathy to suits OTHER than your base's suit. This pumps non-base-suit cards through the discard, biasing the outcast cycle away from your base.
  • Save bird cards for spreads matching your base's suit. This lets you place same-suit sympathy without burning a same-suit supporter — preserving suit-balance.
  • Craft liberally. Each craft cycles cards. The more you craft, the more you push the discard pile in directions that benefit you.
  • Train with bird cards. Train (recruit officer) costs a matching-suit card; bird cards work for any suit. Saving same-suit cards for emergency spreads.

Why you devastate the Lizards in return

  • Revolt kills don't give them acolytes. When you revolt, you remove enemy buildings AND warriors at no acolyte cost to them. You can wipe entire stacks of Lizard warriors and gardens with zero compensation.
  • Sympathy costs them acolytes to even attempt. They need to spend 1–2 acolytes to start a battle on your sympathy clearing — and Guerrilla War means they lose warriors in the fight, with no acolytes returned for those losses.
  • Forced relocations. If they bring warriors from adjacent clearings to attack your sympathy, those clearings now have fewer warriors — opening more opportunities for your sympathy or for other factions to hit them.
  • Bottom line: Lizards run out of acolytes faster than you run out of sympathy.
Risk Other players might try to collude on the outcast suit (e.g., everyone discards a particular suit to force outcast onto your base). But removing your single base while you keep cycling cards is a tall order — and your bird cards give you escape options.

17Glossary

Every term in this aid, defined. Use as a quick reference if any tile uses jargon you don't recognize.

Alliance Pieces & Concepts

Sympathy (token)
A small Alliance token placed in a clearing showing local support for the rebellion. Scores VP based on the sympathy track. Triggers Outrage when removed or battled.
Base
A bigger Alliance building placed via Revolt. Allows you to recruit warriors, train officers, and is required to even play the faction at full strength. You only have 3 (one per non-bird suit). Lose all bases = effectively lose game.
Warrior
Standard Alliance unit. Defends bases and sympathy. Triggers Guerrilla War in combat (you take the higher die when defending).
Officer
A promoted warrior (via Train action). Each officer gives +1 action in Daylight (Battle/Recruit/Organize). Cannot defend like warriors do.
Supporter
A face-down card stored in your supporters stack. Spent to spread sympathy or revolt. Effectively a delayed VP — banking supporters = banking VP for the big turn.

Alliance Actions

Spread
Birdsong action. Pay 1 supporter card matching a clearing's suit (or a bird card) to place sympathy in an adjacent clearing.
Revolt
Birdsong action. Pay 2 supporter cards of a clearing's suit to place a base there. Removes all enemy pieces, recruits warriors (1 + 1 per other Alliance clearing), and scores VP.
Train
Daylight action. Discard a card matching a base's clearing suit to promote 1 warrior at that base into an officer (+1 future Daylight action).
Organize
Daylight action (1 officer-action). Convert one warrior in a clearing into a sympathy token there. Lets you spread without spending supporter cards.
Recruit
Daylight action. Place 1 warrior at each of your bases (no card cost). Replenishes lost defenders.

Special Mechanics

Guerrilla War
Alliance permanent ability. When defending a clearing, you take the HIGHER of the two dice rolled (instead of the lower). Makes attacking you very expensive.
Outrage
Triggered whenever an opponent removes one of your sympathies OR battles in a clearing with your sympathy. They give you a card matching the clearing's suit (or you draw one if it's a bird clearing). Free cards = free future VP.
Martial Law
When a clearing has 3+ enemy warriors, placing sympathy there costs you 1 EXTRA card (so 2 cards total instead of 1). Mostly a Cat-blocker effect.
Favor card
A "Favor of the [suit]" card lets you wipe all enemy warriors and buildings from clearings of a specific suit, scoring big VP. Requires sympathy on multiple clearings of that suit. End-game finishers.

Game Phases

Birdsong
First phase of your turn. Spread sympathy, revolt, and play any bird-cost crafts (e.g. Royal Claim).
Daylight
Middle phase. Craft using sympathy, then take any number of Move/Battle/Recruit/Organize/Train actions (most cost 1 officer).
Evening
Final phase. Draw cards (1 + 1 per craftable-suit sympathized clearing, with the Draw Bonus). Move banked cards into supporters.

Lizard-Specific Terms

Outcast suit
One of the four suits designated each round as "outcast." Lizards get bonuses on outcast-suit clearings (cheaper attacks, better recruits).
Sanctify
Lizard action that replaces an enemy building with a Lizard garden in an outcast-suit clearing. If your base is outcast-suit, sanctify can DELETE your base. Game-ending for you.
Acolyte
Lizard currency piece earned from killing enemy warriors in combat. Spent on sanctify and Lizard powers. Killing Lizards yourself does NOT give them acolytes — Alliance revolt explicitly destroys them without compensation.
Garden
Lizard building (their version of "score a VP each turn"). Built via sanctify. Targetable by your revolts.

Crafting Terms

Item
A face-up token earned from crafting. Some have ongoing effects, some are 1-VP fluff. Vagabond can pick these up — be careful what you craft.
Upgrade (persistent effect)
A crafted card that stays in front of you with an ongoing rules change (Armorers, BBB, Cobbler, etc.). Best Alliance crafts.
Aid
Vagabond action where you give them an item from your crafted stash in exchange for a card. Lets you avoid Hostile relations and gain a card. Use sparingly with weak items.

Common Strategy Terms

"Big turn"
The Alliance's signature win condition: a single explosive turn where you score 10+ VP via spreads, crafts, and organizes. See §05.
Time-bomb revolt
Aggressive sympathy/base placement deep in enemy territory after your first base is secure. See §10.
Coalition
When two or more opponents informally team up against the perceived leader. Your goal: never trigger this by appearing to lead.
